## A journey, not a workshop

The difference matters. A workshop has a beginning and an end on the same day. A journey has phases: it's prepared, executed, processed and edited. Between each phase there's time — to let things settle, to look at the images with distance, to decide calmly.

*Street and gaze* is a 3 to 4-week journey through a Barcelona neighbourhood. Not the neighbourhoods you find in guidebooks. A neighbourhood with its own life, its own rhythms and people who live there — not just pass through.

The result is not a developed roll of film. It's a photobook.

---

## The four phases

### Phase 1 — The place, almost without a camera

The first session isn't about photography. It's about looking.

We go to the chosen neighbourhood for the edit. We walk. We observe the rhythms: what time the market opens, where the elderly sit, where the children play, which shops survive, what the place sounds like. We take notes. We talk about what we see. We try to understand the logic of that urban space.

If someone wants to photograph with their phone or sketch something, go ahead. The analogue camera isn't used in this phase. Not because it's forbidden but because it's better not to have it: it forces you to look differently when you can't shoot.

We end the session with a conversation about what we've seen and what we want to photograph. What story is possible here? What characters do we want to find? What light, what time of day.

### Phase 2 — The execution

We return to the neighbourhood. Now with a camera.

We've spent days thinking about the place. We know where we want to be and at what time. The day has been planned with purpose: not as a rigid plan but as an intention. We go with a loaded roll — or two, if the project calls for it — and an idea of what we're looking for.

Joan accompanies but doesn't direct. Photographic silence is necessary: everyone needs to find their own relationship with the place without interference. We come together to talk about the experience, not to go through frames one by one.

The recommended tool is the Leica M or any analogue rangefinder. The reason isn't snobbery: the silence of the shutter, the size of the camera and the mechanics of pre-focusing change the physical relationship with people. When you're not hiding behind a large SLR, the distance is different.

### Phase 3 — Darkroom: development, contact sheet and scan

We take the negatives to develop. We work together.

While the negatives dry, we talk about the shoot: the moments we lived but didn't photograph, the split-second decisions, what happened between the previous day's intention and the reality of the street.

When the negatives are dry, we make the contact sheet — or scan, if the format requires — and we read it. Not to pick the "good" photos. To understand the body of work: how many images we have, which ones repeat thematically, which ones surprise, which ones confirm what we already expected.

The first edit happens here, but it isn't final. Images need time.

### Phase 4 — Voice, final edit and photobook

The last session is the hardest and the most important.

We bring the pre-selected images. We work on the sequence: the order isn't chronological or thematically mechanical — it's narrative. Which image opens the project? Which closes it? Which ones speak to each other?

We talk about voice: not the "subject" of the work, but the point of view. What is your place in the neighbourhood you photographed? What do you say about that space that no one else could say in quite the same way?

By the end of the session, we have the final sequence. We build it as a digital photobook with **112books** — Llumàtics' editorial project. Format, paper, text if needed. The digital version is the minimum included result. The printed version is optional and priced separately.
